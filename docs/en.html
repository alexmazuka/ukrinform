Why I Built This: An Open Letter from the Former Director General of Ukrinform

Oleksiy Matsuka · Founder, CensorZero · April 2026

Dear colleagues in international media organizations, press freedom advocates, and donor communities,

In November 2023, I was appointed Director General of Ukrinform — Ukraine's national news agency, established in 1918. I came with a reform plan: modernize editorial standards, increase source diversity, strengthen the separation between news and government messaging, and bring Ukrinform closer to the standards expected of a European public broadcaster.

These reforms were met with resistance from parts of the existing staff who had worked under the old model for years. Institutional change is always difficult — especially during wartime, especially at a state-owned media outlet where editorial habits are deeply entrenched.

During my tenure, I received a letter from a government official containing a list of suggested speakers and topics — a routine occurrence for any state news agency. The automated distribution system I had implemented at Ukrinform routed this letter to one of my deputies according to standard workflow. This letter was subsequently used against me internally — as supposed evidence that I was attempting to "introduce censorship." This accusation was factually false: the system I built was designed precisely to make editorial processes transparent and accountable, not to centralize control.

In April 2024, the Institute of Mass Information (IMI) — Ukraine's most prominent media monitoring organization — excluded Ukrinform from its "White List" of transparent and responsible media. The stated reason: "parket messages" (uncritical official coverage) and "insufficient balance of opinions." The timing aligned with the internal campaign against my reform efforts. I left the position one month later.

I was ultimately forced to resign due to pressure from the key actors behind this campaign. I want to state clearly: I never gave editorial instructions to write or not write about any person or topic. This research analyzes 51,589 Ukrinform articles across both periods — 26,342 from my tenure and 25,247 from the period before Ukrinform's return to the White List. Every article is publicly accessible. These data do not support the claim that the editorial output became more "parket" or less balanced under my leadership.

What troubled me was not the criticism itself — media monitoring is essential for a healthy democracy. What troubled me was that IMI's assessment appeared to align with an internal political campaign rather than being based on verifiable data.

IMI evaluated approximately 100 articles over 2 days. They did not publish which articles were assessed, who assessed them, or how individual scores were assigned. Their methodology cannot be independently verified or reproduced.

For nearly two years, I did not have the strength to fight this publicly. The injustice was overwhelming — being accused of the very thing I was trying to reform against. But data doesn't care about politics or timing. Now, with the tools to analyze the full public record, I can finally say with evidence what I could not prove before.

So I decided to do what any journalist would do: check the data myself.

Using Claude Code — an AI development tool by Anthropic — I collected 51,589 Ukrinform articles across both periods (my tenure and the period before Ukrinform's return to the White List in December 2025). I applied algorithmic analysis to the same criteria IMI cited: source balance and "parket" content.

The results are clear:

  • The "parket" rate under my leadership: 6.61%
  • The "parket" rate after my departure: 6.01%
  • Statistical significance: p = 0.007, but effect size Cohen's d = 0.025 — negligible
  • Including military bulletins: 6.51% vs 6.42% — virtually identical

The structure of Ukrinform's content — which officials were covered, how many sources per article, how opposition voices were represented — remained stable across both periods. The data does not support the conclusion that content quality changed with the change of management.

I am not claiming IMI acted in bad faith. I am saying their methodology lacks the transparency required for such consequential assessments. When a monitoring organization's decision can influence the career of a media leader and the reputation of a national news agency — that decision must be verifiable.

This research is fully open:

  • Every article is available for inspection: Message Explorer
  • Every script is open source: GitHub
  • Every change is tracked in Git history — nothing can be quietly edited

I hope this work contributes to a broader conversation about accountability in media monitoring — not just in Ukraine, but globally. Monitoring organizations hold significant power over media reputations. That power must come with transparency.

If you find errors in our analysis, please open an issue on GitHub. If you have questions, I am available to discuss this research openly.

Oleksiy Matsuka
Founder, CensorZero (censorzero.com)
Former Director General, Ukrinform (November 2023 — May 2024)

Transparency Questions

What IMI Has Not Disclosed

These are standard reproducibility requirements for any research methodology.

  1. Which ~100 Ukrinform articles were assessed in each wave?
    IMI does not publish the list. Independent verification is impossible.
  2. Who personally assessed the articles? What is the inter-rater reliability?
    Evaluator composition and agreement rates are not disclosed.
  3. What scores did each article receive on each of the 14 indicators?
    Only the aggregate conclusion is published, not the scoring matrix.
  4. Did the evaluators change between the H1 2024 and H2 2025 waves?
    A change in evaluators could explain different outcomes regardless of media quality.
  5. Why was Ukrinform returned to the White List if our analysis shows no improvement in parket metrics?
    IMI may have factors beyond our algorithm. We await their official response.

Verify Everything Yourself

51,589 articles, collection and analysis scripts, completeness verification report — all publicly available on GitHub. Anyone can download the data, run the scripts, and get the same numbers. Found an error? Open an Issue.

Why This Research Matters

Media monitoring is essential for democracy — especially in a country at war. IMI plays an important role in Ukraine's media ecosystem. But monitoring power must come with accountability.

We don't know exactly what IMI measured, which articles they evaluated, or who assigned the scores. It is a closed process. This research — built with Claude Code by Anthropic — is fully open: every article, every script, every Git commit is public and verifiable.

We hope this work helps IMI become more effective, more transparent, and focused on its mission — not on political games.
