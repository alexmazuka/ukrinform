Open Letter

Open Letter on the Ukrinform Content Research

Oleksiy Matsuka · Founder, CensorZero · April 2026

Dear colleagues, members of the media community, and international partners,

I am publishing this research for one simple purpose: to open the facts for independent verification in a case that had public, professional, and institutional consequences.

In 2023–2024, my work as Director General of Ukrinform became the subject of criticism, including claims of increased "parket" (uncritical official) content and balance issues in the agency's news output. Such assessments matter not only for an individual, but for understanding how serious conclusions about editorial quality and management decisions should be formed in the media environment.

That is why I chose to respond to this case not with polemics, but with open data, transparent analytical logic, and the possibility of external verification.

Why I am publishing this research

Public assessments in the media environment carry weight. They affect trust in institutions, careers, perceptions of reform, and the willingness of partners to work with people and teams in the future.

It is therefore fundamentally important to me that significant conclusions — especially those concerning editorial standards and management quality — rest on approaches that can be verified, reproduced, and critically evaluated.

I collected an open corpus of 51,589 Ukrinform articles across two comparable periods — my tenure (26,342 articles) and a later period after my departure (25,247 articles) — and analyzed the signals publicly cited as grounds for criticism: the share of protocol-official news, source structure, and balance indicators.

The main finding

The open data do not support the claim that during my tenure Ukrinform's content became significantly more "parket" or noticeably less balanced than in the comparable period after my departure.

In other words, the open corpus of articles shows no structural shift that would justify strong, far-reaching conclusions about a fundamental deterioration in news quality during this specific period.

This does not mean that any state media outlet is automatically free from systemic problems. Nor does it mean that algorithmic analysis can replace a full professional editorial review.

But it does mean something else: when serious public claims are made, they must withstand verification against open data.

What this project shows — and what it does not

I deliberately want to define the boundaries of this research.

This analysis does not claim to verify all possible journalism quality standards. It does not assess the completeness of background in each article, does not establish the factual truth of every claim, and does not replace human editorial audit.

Instead, it checks a narrower but fundamentally important question: whether the signals publicly used as arguments for criticism are confirmed in the open corpus of articles.

It is in this area, in my view, that the key conclusion emerges: public assessments of this magnitude must be transparent enough for others to verify.

Why this matters beyond one specific case

I do not view this project as an attempt to revisit a past dispute or continue it in another form.

For me, this is a broader question — about the standard of professional accountability in media.

If a monitoring assessment, editorial conclusion, or professional judgment can affect trust in an institution or a person's professional trajectory, that assessment should be:

  • sufficiently transparent;
  • methodologically clear;
  • open to criticism;
  • suitable for independent reproduction.

In my view, this does not weaken professional monitoring. On the contrary — it makes it stronger, because it shifts the discussion from the level of interpretations and reputational labels to the level of methods, data, and verifiable conclusions.

What this case means for me as a manager

I am publishing this research not to ask for leniency or to replace one set of emotions with another.

I am publishing it because I believe the right way to respond to complex professional cases is through openness to verification, precise articulation of the limits of one's conclusions, readiness for external criticism, and discipline in working with facts.

For me, this is also a management lesson.

Working in media institutions during wartime — under pressure, polarization, and high-speed decision-making — requires not only convictions but very clear procedures: transparent routing of sensitive communications, clear separation between information flows and editorial decisions, and internal mechanisms that minimize the space for misinterpretation and distrust.

Today I look at this case not as grounds for resentment, but as an example of how important transparency, procedural discipline, and independent verification are in media management.

Openness to criticism and verification

This project is published precisely so that it can be checked. The corpus of articles is open. The data processing logic is open. The method's limitations are stated. Space has been left for corrections, criticism, and external review.

I do not propose to treat this research as the final truth. I propose to treat it as an open and verifiable case that allows professional conversation based on data rather than assumptions.

This, in my view, is how trust is built — not through the loudness of claims, but through the quality of the argument and openness to verification.

Oleksiy Matsuka
Founder, CensorZero
Former Director General, Ukrinform (November 2023 — May 2024)

Transparency Questions

What IMI Has Not Disclosed

These are standard reproducibility requirements for any research methodology.

  1. Which ~100 Ukrinform articles were assessed in each wave?
    IMI does not publish the list. Independent verification is impossible.
  2. Who personally assessed the articles? What is the inter-rater reliability?
    Evaluator composition and agreement rates are not disclosed.
  3. What scores did each article receive on each of the 14 indicators?
    Only the aggregate conclusion is published, not the scoring matrix.
  4. Did the evaluators change between the H1 2024 and H2 2025 waves?
    A change in evaluators could explain different outcomes regardless of media quality.
  5. Why was Ukrinform returned to the White List if our analysis shows no improvement in parket metrics?
    IMI may have factors beyond our algorithm. We await their official response.

Verify Everything Yourself

51,589 articles, collection and analysis scripts, completeness verification report — all publicly available on GitHub. Anyone can download the data, run the scripts, and get the same numbers. Found an error? Open an Issue.

Why This Research Matters

Media monitoring is essential for democracy — especially in a country at war. IMI plays an important role in Ukraine's media ecosystem. But monitoring power must come with accountability.

We don't know exactly what IMI measured, which articles they evaluated, or who assigned the scores. It is a closed process. This research — built with Claude Code by Anthropic — is fully open: every article, every script, every Git commit is public and verifiable.

We hope this work helps IMI become more effective, more transparent, and focused on its mission — not on political games.
